CHALLENGE

The challenges was to create an online dashboard design that displayed critical road conditions for officials at the Pennsylvania Department of Transportation (PennDot). Every day, PennDot officials need to stay apprised of changing conditions in the city of Pittsburgh in order to make quick decisions on how to resolve poor road conditions. The dashboard needed to be non-scrolling and non-interactive so that officials could process and understand information at a glance.

PROTOTYPES

By sketching on paper, I focused on grouping information, displaying data, and creating an obvious signal for an emergency situation.

Categorizing the situations into "Openings", "Closings", and "Weather" made it easy to see which action type was associated with the items in the column. I also considered different ways to display information to indicate that an item required no action versus requiring action due to being in an emergency state. From early iterations to later ones, the amount of detailed data provided increases.

The next step was to create mid-fidelity digital sketches in PowerPoint to visualize the colors and text opacity I planned to use. I wanted to use the colors of a traffic light to highlight good and emergency states, while also making emergency state items stand out by making the text more opaque. From class critiques of my hand sketches, I realized that I had been missing small important details for a traffic official, like providing the time and date, so those were added to the digital sketches.

In Illustrator, I began fleshing out a high-fidelity dashboard. In this, I attempted to brand my design more towards being a PennDot product.

This design included elements from my previous sketches, such as the meters and dials, color choices, and column categorizations. However, I realized that the categorizations might not make total sense for someone viewing the dashboard. There were too many colors present with the traffic light colors, so it wasn't as obvious from a glance which items were in an emergency state. From this iteration, I also was not pleased with the design--while it definitely is functional, it looked a lot like an out-of-box solution. I decided to pull more at my design background and challenge myself.

OUTCOME

In the final design, I used color only to make items in an emergency state pop. Including the background view of Pittsburgh with the boxes laid on top adds more visual interest and provides a more modern feel to an otherwise archaic-looking system.
