Traffic Dashboard | UI Design

CHALLENGE

The goal was to create an online dashboard design that displayed critical road conditions for officials at the Pennsylvania Department of Transportation (PennDot). Every day, PennDot officials need to stay apprised of changing conditions in the city of Pittsburgh in order to make quick decisions about setting up detours, opening or closing roads, turning traffic signals on or off, changing the direction of the flow of traffic, and sending out road crews for safety related work. The dashboard needed to be non-scrolling and non-interactive so that officials could process and understand information at a glance.

PROTOTYPES

I started by sketching on paper and focused on how I could group information, display data, and create an obvious signal for an emergency situation. By categorizing the situations into "Openings", "Closings", and "Weather", I aimed to make it easy to see which action type was associated with the items in the column. I also thought of different ways to display information to indicate that an item required no action versus requiring action due to being in an emergency state. From early iterations to later ones, the amount of detailed data provided increases.

I took the next step in creating mid-fidelity digital sketches in PowerPoint to better visualize the colors and text opacity I planned to use. Using color as an indicator of the good and emergency states made items pop more. Changing the opacity of text instructions also aided this pop by having the less opaque text that appeared when an item was in an emergency state catch more attention. In my hand sketches, I realized that I had been missing small important details for a traffic official, like providing the time and date, so those were added.

In Illustrator, I began fleshing out a high-fidelity dashboard. In this, I attempted to brand my design more towards being a PennDot product.

This design included a lot of the elements from my previous sketches, such as a the meters and dials, color choices, and categorizations. However, I realized that the categorizations might not make total sense for someone viewing the dashboard. I also felt that there were too many colors present, so it wasn't as obvious from a glance what condition each item was in--an official would really need to look at each dial and meter individually. From this iteration, I also was not pleased with the design--while it definitely is functional, it looked a lot like an out-of-box solution. From this, I decided to pull more at my design background and challenge myself.

OUTCOME

This is the final design I presented, with both the good state and the emergency state. By only filling in either the good or bad state, I feel that items requiring attention pop more. Including the view of Pittsburgh with the boxes laid on top adds more visual interest and provides a more modern feel to an otherwise archaic-looking system.
