CHALLENGE

Our client, SapientNitro, challenged our team to envision a new food delivery, shopping, preparation, or consumption service that leverages data from separate service ecosystems into our own service or platform. We created Digestible.

Health complications can often be remedied by a change in diet, and patients only see their doctor yearly for check-ups. After a trip to the office, there is nothing holding patients accountable to change their eating habits. Our goal with Digestible was to keep track of a patient's grocery and restaurants receipts and sync them with existing nutrition and fitness applications along with updating the patient's health record that their doctor could see. We aimed to create a service that keeps your doctor in the loop on your health all year and keeps patients accountable for their diets.

CONCEPT EVALUATION

Starting with a concept map outlining the different topics behind food alowed us to discover the space of food education. We initially wanted to provide a solution to childhood obesity

To learn more about the eating habits and behaviors of children, we interviewed the stakeholders who influenced that: a parent, a teacher, a nutritionist, and a school food supplier. We also got the chance to conduct a field study at the Central Kitchen, where all of the food for Pittsburgh's elementary schools is cooked and packaged. From these primary resources, three key insights emerged.

    • KEY INSIGHTS

    1. Access, convenience, and lack of money prevent people from eating healthy.

    2. "Gatekeepers", like parents and schools, have more influence over what children eat than children do.

    3. The school food program does not have a lot of money flowing within the ecosystem. If we were to disrupt this, the economic inflow would have to be from elsewhere.

IDEATION

With the three insights in mind, we used an affinity diagram to brainstorm services that our initial health-conscious parent and busy child personas would benefit from.

Narrowing our ideas to three that we storyboarded and speed dated online via surveys distributed on Reddit and in-person with individuals outside of two Pittsburgh grocery stores to get their thoughts, we decided to focus on the themes of access and knowledge. At this point, the goal of preventing childhood obesity was clearly out of scope for the timeline of the project, and decided to temporarily explore and broaden our scope to a more "help people eat better" before focusing on a new, smaller idea.

Storboarding and speed dating provided two final findings:

  1. Most people we wanted to help did not own smartphones, laptops, or have internet access, so we needed to rethink our target audience because technology is not a solution for everyone.

  2. Though the ideas were well-received, there was a lot of doubt on whether grocery stores would implement the ideas we suggested.

USER ENACTMENT

Speed dating results rated the ideas of grocery store pre-labeling and recipt health scores highest in need and usefulness, so our team performed a user enactment to test these two ideas.

The enactment helped us remember that shoppers make impulse buys. The shopper commented that the shock appeal of a grocery health score might wear out after the first few purchases.

CONCEPT PIVOT

With many new insights and ideas, we recognized a need to narrow our target audience and think about the business value proposition that our service would offer to stakeholders.





A problem space that existed where people were in poor health and had the capacity to improve their health with a small change in diet.

This is when doctors came into the picture and patients emerged from our general target because a macro perspective of food purchases could give a more holistic picture of a person's health. We wanted to provide doctors with year-round health data.

At the same time, we thought about how we could make sense of this data for the consumer who could learn to make changes to their diet. We wanted to help consumers understand their shopping/food purchase behavior and help suggest incremental improvements

From this, three new personas based on three key stakeholders were created: doctor, grocery store/restaurant, and patient/consumer.

SERVICE MODELS

Thinking about the needs and interactions of Dr. Herman, Alan, and Brian in mind, our team modeled the new service in a system map, value flow model, and stakeholder model.

DESIGN

Brainstorming potential user stories helped us recognize what a user would want to do and see in our application, and we decided to use the story of a high-risk individual with heart disease to demonstrate the value of Digestible. We focused on this single workflow during our design phase.

From user testing the screens we sketched, we learned that the strength of our app was in providing users with recommendations of healthy alternatives, and not in displaying data. Behavior is hard to change--it's difficult to tell someone to give up chips and eat broccoli instead. Because of this, we demonstrated less dramatic changes like the substitution of frozen corn for canned corn, which is an easy change for a low-sodium diet.

Branding was important as we conducted a visual language and logo exploration, wishing to put forth a feeling of health and wellness along with the professionalism of a doctor. We decided on the final color scheme and logo with the help of Reddit's votes on what they felt fitted our service.

FUTURE WORK

I learned a lot about how models are a great visual to use during design discussions and to receive critique and thoughts from key stakeholders. If I were able to spend more time working on the project, there are a few design and technical goals I would want to fulfill in order to further enhance the user experience:

  1. Interview doctors and build out their side of the service. We envision building a web interface that would incorporate with existing electronic records, highlighting improvements since the patient's last visit or problem points during the year.

  2. Conceptualize services within grocery shopping data. Ideas that rose during ideation included fulfillment services, like having grocery stores partner with doctors to provide patients with personalized food baskets for their diet needs that could be easily picked up or delivered.

  3. Explore solutions outside of technology. Early in the process, we recognized that not everyone has access to a phone or computer--how can we help these individuals get the information to improve their diets?
